Two-sided model

On one side: supply — listings, marketing, land / project narratives, tools for owners to bring stock. On the other: sales — in-house or affiliated agents who sell and earn commission on closed deals. The platform also provides listing presentation (media, copy, exports) and go-to-market support — not “agents-only marketing themselves” in isolation.

“Whole marketplace” vs narrow lead gen

Ambition is broader than buying a few leads: own discovery, qualification, and handoff so sellers get qualified buyers and buyers get guided service — contrasted with shallow portals that stop at exposure.

AI in the journey

Proprietary AI assists the agent workflow: initial triage of inbound interest, routing to the right agent (geography, capacity, portfolio fit, “who closes this profile best”). Experiments with an AI agent for steps such as visit scheduling and follow-up if the human agent is unavailable — always with escalation paths.

Intelligence layer vs judgment work

Framework from the conversation: much operational work is instruction-following (scheduling, data entry, standard responses); high-value work is judgment — reading intent, emotion, risk, and negotiation. AI should absorb the former so humans spend more time on the latter. The stack evolves continuously as models improve (“re-optimise” processes every few months).

Scale: Switzerland vs China reference

Contrast with markets of hundreds of thousands of agents on a single platform (China example in the dialogue): Switzerland’s model is smaller headcount, higher touch. Scale is sought through platform leverage and efficiency tools, not infinite hiring.

Process innovation vs “one person holds the whole deal”

Discussion of alternative commission models that split the transaction into roles (open house, contract prep, closing) vs traditional sole agent. Swiss operator is cautious: they optimize steps but are not yet fully “unbundled”; growth focus includes digitization and selective automation rather than only headcount.

Future of agencies

Thesis: small brokerages that adopt platform + technology can outperform those that ignore tooling; large incumbents also adapt. Not everyone needs to be a generalist — specialists (closers, listing experts) may coexist. Human role remains strong for trust on high-stakes, emotional purchases (home, life savings); documentation and admin should automate as much as possible.

Portals and content (US angle)

Brief exchange on whether US-style players advertise listings they host vs using inventory to acquire users — context for how marketplace brokerage differs from pure media portals.

Audience Q&A (from memo)

Agency of the future — traditional firms vs individual shift?

Expect hybrid: platforms that bundle tech for many small agents, plus expert humans for negotiation and trust-heavy moments. Technology literacy becomes baseline for survival.

Automation vs human touch?

For primary residence, people still want a confidant / guide through a stressful process; the opportunity is to remove boring work so the human can play that role. For other asset classes the balance may differ.
