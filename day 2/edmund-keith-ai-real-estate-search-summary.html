So, where are we with AI real estate search?

Presentation — As Ed’s data shows, AI is the idea that has come to dominate industry narratives and valuations. The discourse around it isn’t always grounded in what’s actually shipping.

From deck — Four key takeaways

Slide: AI icon = asterisk (what we think we know vs reality).

  1. Trust what companies are doing over what experts like me are saying.
  2. If you don’t have semantic image indexing, you don’t really have AI search yet.
  3. Portals aren’t going anywhere.
  4. The implementation will be as messy and varied as the world’s real estate markets.

From deck — “Slap an LLM over filters”

Slide title: SLAP AN LLM OVER FILTERS — PEOPLE CAN SEARCH CONVERSATIONALLY

Mockup (Immoweb): Traditional filters (transaction type, countries, postal codes, property types) plus a conversational chat panel.

Example dialogue:

  • User: “houses for sale in Brussels 2–4 beds, garden/pool”
  • AI: “I’ve found 154 houses for sale in Brussels. While I don’t see specific filters for gardens/pools, you still have a great selection of properties to explore. Would you like to refine your search further, perhaps by specifying a budget or the number of bedrooms?”
  • CTA: “See 154 results” · Input placeholder: “Describe your dream home…”

Point: You can add conversational search on top of existing filters, but the AI is limited by the underlying filter set (e.g. no garden/pool filter → AI can’t fulfil that part of the query).

Talk summary — voice transcription (ASR cleaned)

Edmund Keith · PPW Bangkok · ~20 min. Editor of Online Marketplaces 6 years; ex-aggregator (formerly Zoopla Group). Opened with joke: asked an LLM to add three credentials → “world-renowned expert on AI search” — don’t trust experts blindly.

Utopia for Realists & the fourth idea

Concept from Rutger Bregman: society needs to embrace big utopian ideas that seem unachievable. Slide listed female suffrage, gay marriage, 15-hour work week — and a fourth, not in the book: matching people with a home without showing them irrelevant premium listings at the top of search. Ed as “someone on the sidelines” can champion that.

Overton window

Overton window = the band where an idea moves from unthinkable/radical to acceptable. You move it by discussing it or by doing it. Example he used: portal monetization tactics. Policy = “everybody does it” (e.g. mortgage leads). Acceptable = e.g. % of commission to sell. Radical = e.g. pay-for-advertising. Unthinkable = e.g. becoming an actual brokerage. (Later he caveats: FSBO isn’t policy everywhere — forbidden in UK, Japan, Australia; pay-for-ad isn’t radical in Sweden/Australia; iBuying still happens in Turkey — so “portals” aren’t one block; view through your market.)

AI dominates the narrative

NLP analysis of all content they’ve put out + keywords from conferences: AI has gone from buzzword to “very big deal” over the last few years. Overton for AI ideas: policy = “AI is a big deal,” “AI is enabler not threat.” Acceptable = “we don’t have to panic; LLMs won’t put portals out of business.” (He “exclusively reveals” he asked two LLMs trained on their content whether they’d take over — they said no.) Radical = making portals “content makers for an OpenAI interface.” Unthinkable = “just take over and assign everybody a home.” He wrote “Five reasons ChatGPT isn’t going to take over real estate search” — got pushback (emails, comments). Net: uncertainty around these ideas is still very high. Sentiment in their content around “AI” = mostly opportunity but a big chunk uncertainty, and uncertainty isn’t dropping. His own writing/speaking is “very uncertain.”

Don’t trust experts — including him

Showed slide: him in Barcelona confidently saying natural language search would go voice-assisted; one year later saying he preferred conversation. Same for other experts: he asked an LLM for contradictory quotes from various speakers and “it does a very good job.” What moves ideas through the Overton window is people doing things, not people saying things — e.g. New Zealand gave women the vote in 1893, then everyone did it. So to see where we are with AI search, look at what portals are doing.

What portals are doing — the numbers

At start of 2025, ~10 portals had claims to AI search. That has “more than doubled” since (so ~24–25 when he made the slide). “The vast majority of people looking for a new home are not looking at any kind of AI-powered search.” Among the ~25, there’s a very broad spectrum of what the claim means.

Bottom rung: “Slap an AI logo”

See what people search for, slap an LLM over existing filters — conversational front end. He tested two big portals who’d done that: the only accurate answers were for parameters they have filters for. Immoweb (Belgium, now in Melbourne): looks like “sensational ChatGPT-style” search, but ask for same neighbourhood, countertops, kitchen island, double garage, etc. — “it has no idea.”

Next rung: AI-powered filters

Using AI to improve filter-based search (e.g. 99.co, Zoopla — tags/labels from descriptions). “Less disingenuous”; they’re not reinventing the wheel, just improving what they’ve had for 20 years. “Fair enough,” “a bit interesting.”

Where it gets good: visual semantic index

Gy (person who built it possibly in audience): has visual semantic indexing — “actually knows what’s inside the home.” Example: “homes for sale with granite countertops” → accurate results and thumbnails show the granite countertops so you can verify. He ran 420 tests. The most common and most annoying error: portals dropping a parameter because they didn’t know — “almost always things that are in the house that you could see if you did visual semantic index.” Example: “two bedroom houses with high ceilings” → results for two-bed only, “high ceilings” ignored. Gy also does well on neighbourhood / environment (e.g. “houses near school with low noise pollution”). “This is gold. Please everybody aim for this.”

Output format vs accuracy

He tried to be objective in testing. Subjective take: people say we’re at an inflection point (conversational, WhatsApp, “search results don’t look like search results”). There was no correlation between input/output format and accuracy — “it made no difference.” Another subjective view: search results pages have been homogenized and optimized by product managers for 20 years; they display the right amount of information. “Search results pages are like crocodiles — they don’t need to evolve. They’re perfect. Stop messing with them.”

Incredible conclusion metaphor (spoken)

Two ways of doing AI search. Flashy way: conversational search on top without upgrading the data — “Porsche body on a steam engine.” Fine for simple queries (beds, location, price) — “most of the time you do the speed limit.” But as soon as someone asks for kitchen island, double garage, etc., you’ve created expectations you can’t fulfil and done “damage to the brand.” Better way: Gy — no conversational interface, even “full bore on the outside,” but proper engine; user searches for “apartment, ground floor, disabled access” and doesn’t get disappointed.

Overton slide — where AI search is now

Policy: we have filters, everybody likes filters. Acceptable: “chats that work” (finally, in the last few weeks). Popular: “telling investors we’re doing something about it.” Radical (for now): semantic index of images (what’s inside the home, in index space); great neighbourhood / environment data (like Gy). He wants those two to move from radical to acceptable.

Final caveat

Portals aren’t one homogeneous block. Implementation will be as messy and varied as the world’s real estate markets. When you hear anyone (including him) talk about “portals” as one group, take it with a pinch of salt and view through your market.
