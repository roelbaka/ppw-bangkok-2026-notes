What is actually changing then?

  1. How queries are formed — Queries are becoming longer and more diverse. Long tail is growing at unprecedented rates.
  2. Which sources get surfaced — There are more surfaces appearing for searches than ever before.
  3. How need is satisfied pre-click — Queries are being satisfied before users reach your site.
  4. What it takes to be reference-worthy — LLMs, whilst reliant on the search engines, have their own ways of ranking sources.

A new discoverability framework

Three layers from the deck:

Layer 1 — Traditional search
  • Google ranking
  • SEO
  • Internal filters
  • Structured inventory
Layer 2 — Answer layer
  • AI Overviews
  • Featured answers
  • Zero-click extraction
  • Summarised content
Layer 3 — Generative discovery
  • ChatGPT
  • Gemini
  • Perplexity citing
  • Google ranking and recommending search

Portals need to show up across all three layers.

Layer 3: Generative discovery — what it looks like in ChatGPT/Gemini

Example query on slide: Where are the best places to live in Madrid for a young family? Mock-up showed an AI-style interface with sources, images, a text answer recommending neighbourhoods, and a small chart.

Layer 3 — Generative discovery: LLMs citing your content. ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity recommend visitors. Only unique, proprietary content gets cited. This is the moat.

What’s required to compete at each layer?

Three-tier pyramid from the deck — progression: Be crawlable → Be extractable → Be irreplaceable.

  • Layer 1 — Technical SEO (dark blue base): Core Web Vitals, indexation, internal linking, structured markup. Necessary, but not sufficient. Callout on slide: Most marketplaces are here.
  • Layer 2 — Answer-ready data (middle): Enriched, structured content that Google extracts for AI Overviews and snippets. Examples: median prices, school ratings, suburb stats, Schema markup.
  • Layer 3 — Citation-worthy (red top): Proprietary data and insights that LLMs can cite and competition does not have.

Your proprietary data is the only thing that moves you up. That’s the moat.

If AI answers the question, is your brand in the answer?

Discovery traffic isn’t blog traffic. It’s new buyers. And which portal to use.

  1. Buyer asks AI — e.g. “Best suburbs for families in Sydney?”
  2. AI answers with sources — Will the source be Domain, realestate.com.au, or nobody?
  3. User visits the portal named — Impression → direct visit → linking intent → SEO.
The real question: When AI answers a question, are you the source it cites? If not, it’s an opportunity for the competition to take away your market share.

The goal isn’t to stop AI from answering. It’s to make sure your portal is in the answer.

Four-step strategic playbook

From the deck: Protect SEO → Know exposure → Weaponise data → Redefine success.

Step 1 — Protect the existing SEO engine

Do not neglect SEO fundamentals. Focus on search intent: inventory and location page visibility. Technical: crawlability, taxonomy, internal linking, structured data. SEO gives the highest ROI and underpins the answer layer and generative discovery.

24.5% of searches are now zero-click. SEO must now optimize for the answer layer.

Step 2 — Know your traffic mix and content exposure

Understand your overall traffic mix, then classify which content and search traffic is most vulnerable.

Your traffic mix: Organic search (Google, Bing), Direct / brand, AI chatbots (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini), Paid search / social.

Content vulnerability:

Step 3 — Turn your data into a strategic growth asset

Weaponise your proprietary market insights. This is the moat no AI tool can replicate.

  • Unique data & metadata — Unstructured feeds with pricing, features, agent info, availability, and more beyond what any scraper can assemble.
  • Suburb & location intelligence — School ratings, commute times, demographics, lifestyle profiles, crime data, local amenities.
  • Market insights & trends — Price indices, growth %, rental yields, vacancy rates, supply–demand dynamics, transaction histories.
  • Trust & authority signals — Author attribution, update dates, schema markup, expert reviews, agent endorsements.

Talk summary

From voice/ASR transcript — cleaned for readability. Mike opens with: lots of fear about AI taking traffic, not enough clarity; goal is a bit of framework and what portals can actually do.

Opening frame

Is AI search about to disrupt property portals? The truth is more nuanced. Need to separate noise from signal — look at actual data and see which signals matter.

Is AI taking all the traffic?

First slide: Zillow, Rightmove, REA — how much of their traffic is still organic search vs AI referral? We see 0.15%, 0.2%, 0.12%. Not that much. So a lot of this is fear of AI; this is a snapshot, not a forecast. We need to look at trends.

Search volume and where the drop goes

Average searches per US desktop user (Oct 2024–Dec 2025): dropped from ~121 to ~95 per month. People are starting to use AI for discovery. Fewer searches → fewer chances for your site to appear. When we break down where that 21% drop goes: orange = stayed on Google, green = clicked out (organic), yellow = paid, red = searched again, blue = searched and got an answer with no click. Around 25% of searches globally now end with no click — people find the answer in AI Overviews or similar and leave. What you can take solace in: the share that still use organic search to click through to a website has stayed roughly unchanged at ~41%. So in the short term the threat of AI replacing your traffic is a bit overstated; organic hasn’t collapsed. But the bar that’s growing is the one that’s gone from ~0.01% to ~0.06%.

Google AI Mode and the plateau paradox

Google AI Mode launched May 2025 at 0.01% of searches; now 0.06% — six times growth in eight months. Small share but 252 million visits a month. The bigger long-term threat than ChatGPT or Perplexity is Google becoming an AI engine. AI tools are not here to route people to your site; they’re here to answer the question. Chart: orange bars = AI visits; black line = how many of those visits clicked out to a website. We hit an inflection around October; for the last few months AI referrals have been declining. Claude has cut outbound links sharply; Perplexity has halved theirs. More AI visits does not mean more referrals.

Signals combined

20% fewer searches per user; 25% of searches end with no click; AI Mode still small but 6× growth and 252M/month. Once users are in AI, they tend to stay. Each signal on its own is manageable; together they compound. The destruction isn’t in volume yet — it’s in interface and behaviour. By the time you notice the drop in traffic, you’re already behind.

What is actually changing?

Four things: (1) How queries are formed — longer, more diverse, long tail growing. (2) More surfaces — AI Overviews, SGE, Reddit, reviews inside platforms. (3) Need satisfied pre-click — if you’re not showing up in the answer, Google can answer without you. So it’s not “do I get the click?” but “is my data in the AI result?” (4) What it takes to be reference-worthy — who does the LLM cite?

Property journey and who wins where

Discovery (best suburbs, lifestyle) and research (market trends, comparisons) = AI disrupted. Property search (listings, filters) and contact = portal territory. The LLM still needs someone to supply the information; if you don’t, you’re not visible in AI. Panel data: discovery — 35% say AI most useful vs 13% Google; by the decision stage the gap nearly closes. It’s not your inventory traffic at risk; it’s the discovery traffic that shapes the shortlist. If you’re not in the citation, you’re not in the shortlist. The real question: when AI answers, is your portal in the answer? If the buyer asks AI and AI mentions Domain, realestate.com.au, or nobody — if nobody, you get no direct visits or leads. Goal: make sure your portal is in the answer.

Three layers and what to do

Layer 1: traditional search (SEO, indexation). Layer 2: answer layer (AI Overviews, etc.) — sits on top of organic. Layer 3: generative discovery (ChatGPT, Perplexity). Most portals are strong on layer 1 and still weak on 2 and 3; you need the foundations before you can win at layer 3. Example: “two-bed Melbourne” — layer 1 is classic SERP; layer 2 is what data you make extractable; layer 3 is what gets cited (market reports, suburb profiles, not just listing feeds). The moat is proprietary data no AI has. So: technical SEO; answer-ready content (what questions do people ask, do you have data?); citation-worthy content (unique data competitors don’t have). If your content isn’t indexable you won’t get into the answer layer; if you’re not in the answer layer you won’t get cited.

Four-step playbook and closing

Protect existing SEO; know your exposure (traffic mix, which content is vulnerable); weaponise your data; redefine success (visibility across surfaces, citations, share of voice, not just organic clicks and keywords). Closing: AI hasn’t replaced Google yet — overstated. What’s understood: it’s a shift in behaviour, not just volume. What’s underestimated: the three layers of discoverability and how to influence them.

Audience Q&A — questions and answers

From live Q&A and app. Answers paraphrased from transcript.

What determines being ranked high in conversational LLM search results vs. organic Google results? Is it sheer volume of blog articles with the right keywords? Any magic tricks?

No magic tricks. What the models base it on: who’s got the right information, who covers the topics specifically, and who is actually being cited on other websites. Like the Travelmath example — if you’re really good and nobody else has it, you have a very good opportunity to get cited. It’s about quality of the content and how extractable that information is to the model.

During the emergence of Google we learned that customer experience drives users, not brand. Inktomi powered 80% of search via AOL, Yahoo — but everyone remembers Google. Why is it different now?

It’s almost coming full circle. With organic search you had all these “free” results. What’s happening now with AI engines is that brands are becoming important again. When LLMs first came out it was “everyone’s equal,” we can all get cited. In reality it was grounded in where you ranked in search. What we’re seeing now: if you’ve got a bigger brand and the same content, you’re favoured over a smaller player. So if you’re a portal creating content, you have a much stronger position than if a single agent does it. Brand is becoming more and more important.

Whilst searches per user are dropping, is that a false positive as users get better answers (e.g. via integrated Gemini) faster?

Anecdote: in Google AI Mode it used to answer and you could click through to the link. What AI Mode has been doing for the last several days — it gives you the AI answer and you’re in AI Mode. If you want to click the link it takes you to another Google search result, not out to your website. So they’re keeping users in their environment.

Your tone / threat assessment has shifted markedly since Madrid. What are the biggest shifts that have shaped your updated perspective?

It’s not that traffic has disappeared — people are still searching. The threat is what’s going on on the right hand side: where the answer is being supplied. So it’s not that people are searching less; it’s where aren’t they searching and how are they getting their answers? That’s the threat if you’re not aware of where that’s happening. Search volume is still up from the last presentation; Google search is still up. ChatGPT and others aren’t taking search volume away — they’re servicing different parts of the journey. If you’re not across that, that’s where the threat is.
