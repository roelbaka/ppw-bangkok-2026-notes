Talk summary — voice transcription (ASR cleaned)

Panel: Mathew Heywood (NeuralIndex), Edmund Keith, Simon Baker. Theme: retrofitting AI into existing portal experience — conversational search, "provisions for AI search."

What does NeuralIndex do? (Mathew)

"We build AI products on top of property data. We are basically rich cards for portals." They run a service that takes a portal's data and enriches it with on- and off-platform datasets to enable semantic search. The biggest challenge in the AI era: portals have enormous datasets (millions of listings, cities, etc.); when somebody searches, surfacing the right results is hard. NeuralIndex enriches that data and makes it searchable.

What is AI search? (Ed)

It comes down to user expectation. If you say "AI," users expect a "wow moment" like ChatGPT. When a portal says "we have AI search," the experience has to live up to that. First time he was genuinely wowed: computer vision that could identify something in a kitchen (e.g. granite countertops) — "that was when I was like, okay, this is actually AI." AI has come to mean many things; in that sense it also means expectation.

Velocity vs session time (panel)

AI can increase the speed to result — "get to the answer they want faster." But it's controversial: increasing velocity to result doesn't necessarily decrease session time; if users find a result quicker, they may scroll more. So it can be "negative" for engagement at this point. Property search isn't transactional — it's emotional: "I've got my furniture, my expectations, my family… I've never known many people in on Monday and bought by Friday." So "velocity" matters more for some segments (e.g. investors, short-term rentals) than for long discovery cycles.

US and data (Simon / Ed)

US is interesting: same listing data, same feeds — so no content advantage, you compete on user experience. Zillow 20 years ago went down the path of layering crime stats, insurance, etc., so consumers could do a deeper level of query. "So the US was 20 years ago, short [version]."

Disabled-friendly as the test (Ed)

When he feels himself getting carried away by "we have all these data sets," he brings himself back to disabled-friendly. "If you need it, you really need it. Real estate portals do such a bad job — 'we have a disabled-friendly filter' — usually it's not very good, and usually that's the tag that agents game." Where he wants this to go: the portal itself makes a judgment on whether a property is disabled-friendly, regardless of what the agent claims. So don't get carried away by "no crime neighborhood" (what does that mean?) — "being able to show somebody who needs disabled-friendly and actually [only show] disabled-friendly is what's exciting. If you're having trouble conceptualizing what we're talking about, that's what to think about."

It's not a search problem — it's data and business model (Simon / Mathew)

"It's not a search experience [problem]. It's a data and business model problem." There must be a "golden ratio": what % of listings get top-page visibility? If the average search goes from 25 results to 5, what's the acceptable loss of visibility for advertisers? Some traffic will go to "adjacent" listings (99%, 98%, 70% matches). Ed pushed back: in a utopia you'd get five perfect results — but "aren't you only showing five? I feel like missing out on the sixth that may be adjacent." "You don't know what you don't know." Discussion of hard vs soft parameters.

Homegate example (Switzerland)

A platform (e.g. Homegate) does "always on" search: you define hard and soft parameters; instead of showing a list it "goes off and emails you when there are results" and gives a confidence score based on how well results match hard vs soft. Next evolution: at viewing — "these are my hard parameters, these are my soft."

Objective vs subjective; strong / medium / weak matches

Property search is a series of tradeoffs (budget, when to sell, school, size) — "usually arrived at by crunch time." How do you code emotional decisions? Objective = where is it, does it have three beds — not up for debate. Subjective = "I want a modern kitchen" — what's modern to you? So you weight results: strong matches, medium matches, weak matches, and be transparent. "You never want 10 parameters and get one listing — you want 20 results that hit different combinations of the 10. Seven out of 10 might actually be better than the one that hits all 10."

AI as parallel; filters don't go away

Portals "put all the data in and said go" — people search differently (filters, favorites, natural language). "Everyone does it slightly differently — one problem, multiple solutions." So AI or an agentic layer — "AI sort of lives around the outside" — you can do it this way or that. For a while it's a parallel existence; over time as AI gets better on results it will "just become search." Filters won't go away — "slowly transition."

Retrofitted portals — can they do it? (Ed)

Agentic/agent-type solutions — will people trust portal brands or go to ChatGPT? "Probably retrofitted — Homes.com etc. We're in the middle of this. Share price found this [AI narrative] — they're retro designing. I think we underestimate how good [portals] are — they have really good tech; I've been looking at these situations for five years, I'd be quite embarrassed [by some]. They have such good in-house talent — I just can't see them losing out." So yes, retrofitted — "they just need the talent." What's holding them up is not portals themselves — it's expectations from investors. Eventually investors will catch on.

Challenger portals (Simon)

"People who say LLMs will completely [displace] — I don't challenge that [sceptically]. It's such an easy soundbite — I don't see it." Conversation: have challenger portals ever won on user experience? "Very short list… in Italy, for example, but never due to user experience — it's distribution, marketing." Zillow 2004/5: premise was "how much is your house worth" — everything else was behind. Then Zillow said "we'll tell you what the house is worth" — that was an untapped need. "That's user experience, but it's not good search UX — it's the how much, it's a side door."

Challenges of retrofit — missing infrastructure (Ed)

"It's not just put a mic and say 'number four.'" You're missing "fundamental infrastructure to support that experience." In his testing, 22 portals have "performed dramatically better" when they have the right foundations — semantic index of images, neighbourhood data, two-sided conversation, ability to communicate with that data.

Attention spans / digital overload?

Q: Will decreasing attention spans change property search? Ed: "My opinion is property is too important. But I'm the guy who talks to portals — my opinion is framed by who I am. No one's talking to 20-year-olds in the audience — we may be wrong."

AI importance by segment (Mathew)

Default is primary dwelling — but there's rentals, shared accommodation, investment. As you go through that spectrum, AI becomes more or less important. For investors, AI is critical: scan properties, data, estimated rental yield, capital growth — "pure data play," do it across residential/commercial, likelihood of yield, how fast it's selling. Short-term / sharing: speed is important (tight timeline, 2% vacancy — "you're not hanging around for deep analysis over six months"). Primary dwelling: more emotional, exploration. New developments: somewhere in between, more data-driven (terms, financing).

Agents and LLMs — who owns the conversation? (Mathew)

"Really nuanced." NeuralIndex has elements that could redirect to non-portal business. It will matter "when the stock isn't available for [portal]-style information." Key challenge for traditional portals: remain relevant in the conversation. It's not about "LLM bypassing search" — it's "LLM bypassing each interaction." Risk: over time, user has an AI pilot that does search, guides them, then "I'm interested — set up a viewing." How will it contact the agent? Through portal channels or around? Agents have contact details everywhere. So portals need to be in the flow — "instead of going to [portal] I searched 15,000 to find the one" — help people research, be in the consideration set, build trust. "Probably safe space right now."
