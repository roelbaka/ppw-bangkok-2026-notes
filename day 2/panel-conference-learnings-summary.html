Talk summary — voice transcription (ASR cleaned)

Closing panel: Malcolm Myers (eiv), Jonathan Turpin (AIM Group), Simon Baker (Online Marketplaces). ~48 hours of PPW on AI and transactions (not only buyers/sellers marketing). Open Q&A. Speaker tags below are best-effort from audio — correct if your recording differs.

Framing

Synthesis of what changed in the room: focus on AI across the transaction and operations, not a narrow marketing-only lens. Audience invited to surface “random” questions — panel reflects on patterns across sessions.

Malcolm Myers — AI “super-users” and culture

One blunt takeaway: people who are effective AI supervisors / power users are massively more productive. Practical advice for anyone in the room: surround yourself with AI-native operators (and become one if you can) — it will accelerate the business. Anecdotes about everyday life (e.g. trades, kitchen-table tasks) show how fast norms are shifting — but it still feels early.

Org / product culture: Many “new minds” now sit at serious technical depth; product leadership must absorb cultural change, not only tools. Parallel to the newspaper → digital transition — incumbents who knew what was coming and lined up the pieces fared differently. Reference to conversations with industry leaders who are building for the wave even when imperfect today.

Legacy + speed: Large portals have complex stacks and sign-off; meanwhile small teams ship on weekends with AI-assisted coding. Plausible future: old core still running, young teams bolting on AI experiments — micro releases and daily learning rather than one big “macro” replatform. Nordic / other markets already living weekend builder → Monday strategy tension.

Jonathan Turpin — data beats “LLM alone” for search

Across many sessions, the through-line is how much data portals already have or can acquire — and how essential that is for search that actually works. He leaves PPW more convinced than three years ago that raw LLMs are not enough for quality results: data capability in the portal’s hands is the strategic moat. UX ambition only converts if the underlying data layer is serious.

Tension: traffic / LLMs vs exclusive content

Contradiction surfaced in conference debates (e.g. Rhys / Ryan thread): on one hand you may need to expose exclusive content to win positioning and traffic in LLM-mediated discovery; on the other, strategy may call for keeping proprietary inventory and signals “under glass.” Possible resolution sketched as layered rules — what you expose at discovery vs what stays in authenticated / marketplace layers; speed to capture intent, then pull into controlled transaction environments.

Simon Baker — “replay” of portals vs Google; Facebook; generations

Déjà vu: Feels like 2004–2016-era fear when Google could “own” the user — portals worried about disintermediation. Story of listings on Google Maps — industry calls (e.g. Gary Shields named in anecdote): will you let this happen? Lesson: experience and response mattered as much as raw distribution.

Facebook / social: Conference may still under-weight how “normal” massive social graphs are for discovery — tip of the iceberg vs what happens in DMs and groups. Example of a large community (hundreds of thousands) where only a small active core posts, yet that core still drives network effects. AI-generated content makes users more suspicious.

Regional split: Facebook as commerce/discovery layer is a bigger phenomenon in some Asian markets than in Europe/US — maturity and habit differ; in developed markets users may prefer a trusted aggregator/portal. Thesis: portals that invest in trust, security, and brand can still win share back over time — others may disagree. Hard to read Facebook Marketplace revenue vs US portals on the same axis because monetization models differ (transactions vs ads vs off-platform developer spend).

Generations & skepticism: Younger buyers may reject listings that “look AI” (e.g. NYC rental search — fake-enough imagery → skip). High-stakes purchase vs rental behaves differently. Skepticism extends to engagement tricks — “smell test” / common sense. Longer-term: will AI agents fill gaps for the biggest financial decision? Will younger cohorts buy less (economics, savings, rent-with-parents)? Housing and generational wealth themes woven in.

Voice & paradigm shift: Claims he was skeptical on voice but spent extended time on a Homes.com-style conversational experience — speed of answers and navigation impressed; imagines a five-year-old with a screen. Voice + multimodal may be a real shift in how people search property (not “the whole answer” but a first step in new navigation). Specialist depth behind the AI layer still matters — analogy to hunting / discovery vs sterile one-click checkout (different categories and psychologies).

Mobile precedent: Industry once assumed “digital = desktop”; mobile won. AI may become the default service layer across touchpoints — open questions are how value and margin accrue and how vertical stacks stay defensible.

Employment, revolution vs evolution, vertical value

We rarely know the next big thing until it hits — but AI is hard to ignore. Debate whether change is evolutionary or a revolution on par with pre-digital → digital; panel leans that for individual life experience (e.g. post–ChatGPT decade) the shift could feel larger than the early “digital news” era. Employment and social consequences may dwarf narrow property-tech worries.

Property vertical: Not obvious that real estate becomes a low-margin passive sector — complexity of buying a home endures. Monetization changes (performance, new bundles) but deep vertical businesses that help buyers through complexity remain valuable. Underlying human needs and purposes of transacting stay; how we transact changes with AI.

At a glance

  • Hire / become AI-power users — productivity gap is real.
  • Data > naked LLM for search quality — moat is proprietary capture & structure.
  • Expose vs protect content in an LLM world — design layers, don’t pretend there’s one rule.
  • Social / Facebook still under-discussed vs true share of “where people find property.”
  • Trust, brand, anti-AI-slop UX — especially for high-stakes buyers.
  • Voice / conversational search — credible paradigm shift; specialist backends still matter.
  • Legacy stack + fast experiment teams — practical coping pattern for large portals.
  • Revolution framing — AI’s societal impact may dwarf property-only concerns; vertical complexity keeps portals relevant if they adapt.
