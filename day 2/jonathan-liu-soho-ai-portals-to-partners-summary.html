Talk summary — voice transcription (ASR cleaned)

Fireside with Jonathan Liu (Soho.ai). Interviewer not named in transcript. Source: voice memo; phrasing reconstructed where audio was garbled.

Personalization, not one generic AI

Emphasis on sophisticated, personalized technology rather than a single undifferentiated experience. Positioning involves coalition / multi-sided dynamics: not fully dependent on one party; Soho also provides capabilities to others. Value in aggregating data and requirements in one place (attention, subscriptions, extensions) so the stack can grow with additional integrations.

Trust, agents, and a controlled AI engine

In markets framed as “the West”, trust is central. Roots described as connecting middle agents into an AI-powered experience — an AI engine that remains controlled (governed, not a free-for-all). Thinking beyond search and beyond what typical ads or surface-level portal UX show.

Tool across the web, not only the portal

Product vision sketched as a tool that works across content: the portal, social, business contexts, and identity — with the user choosing how they want output (e.g. chat vs structured views like charts). Market volatility noted as a backdrop. A layered model: base experience plus premium offerings on top.

Copilot and browser / extension

Strong thread on an AI copilot in a browser-based experience — not only inside one site. Distinction drawn between raw algorithm and the gaps a product fills; whether “search” or “AI search,” what matters is utility in the workflow — models still err but are improving.

Extension analogy (interviewer): Like shopping helpers that scan pages, surface price/discount patterns, and capture affiliate value — could Soho be a browser extension on major property sites, reading the page, acting as a copilot while you browse, even using camera on a listing photo? Response direction: third-party + combined workflows are on the roadmap of considerations; the hard part is the full buyer journey end-to-end.

The unsolved buyer journey

Today’s email alerts are relatively modular / basic. The opportunity: AI that does research and analysis behind the scenes, not only notifications — how close to transaction, and how much trust users grant. Tagline-style idea: “AI as your copilot for serious property” — install, work with the user, learn over time.

Who pays vs who the product serves

Tension named explicitly: advertisers often pay the bills, but buyers need an advocate for due diligence and navigation. Building something that can be separate from pure advertiser logic — working on behalf of the buyer — could unlock wallet spend (e.g. serious buyers paying for depth). Competing head-on with massive brands on the whole market is hard; alternative is carving foundation segments, higher-value stages, and real differentiated value while spend intensity stays comparable to other channels.

AI-native company

Soho describes itself as AI-native. AI used across the business — not only engineering: marketing, content generation, operations. If a tool doesn’t exist, build it. Broader point: many firms struggle not from lack of desire but from siloed legacy culture; true AI-native means marketing, HR, contracts, etc. informed by AI, starting from basics not only “moonshots.”

Go-to-market

Still need to be in market and close to a viable product; distribution via partners / channels; finding the “Swiss cheese” — where you can win — is ongoing. AI makes some conversations easier with customers. Worries should focus on what you can’t control; make peace with the rest.

Audience Q&A — commercial vs residential

Airline copilot / narrative: Discussion of whether story is headline vs measurable asset-class articulation — intent to ground in technology strengths and trailblaze where possible.

Residential vs commercial: Everyone talks residential; global value may sit more in commercial — deeper data, analytics, becoming the repository for commercial intelligence, landlord and institutional customers, while monetizing via residential-side advertising and related products. Commercial incumbents often stuck in traditional models — room for tools that help investment and decisions over a 5–10 year horizon; unlock may be larger than in residential alone. Transparency demands differ (e.g. large empty tower vs consumer listing).

Focus in a high-opportunity era

Disruption today is faster than a generation ago; many opportunities create a focus problem and context-switching cost. Closing banter ties to attention / “ADHD” metaphor (e.g. Elon Musk) — successful operators still need prioritization discipline.
