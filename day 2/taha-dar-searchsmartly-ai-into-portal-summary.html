  • Building blocks: Understand the seeker · Understand location & neighbourhood · Respect the business / advertiser side.
  • Ideal experience: Personalised match under constraints; reliable, grounded recommendations; explainability (life decisions need “why”).
  • Capture intent: Forms, NL/conversational flows, likes/saves, dwell time on listings and images → inferred preferences.

Framing

Move from buzzword to blueprint: what does it take to ship AI in search? The bottleneck is often data — having the right facts in a form models and rules can use — not only the chat layer on top.

What counts as AI here

Beyond generative UI: NLP for query understanding, computer vision on listing photos, speech in apps, etc. Property search is a multi-modal problem — text, images, maps, behaviour.

North star for AI-powered search

Help each user find a strong match given budget, beds, garden, kitchen, commute (e.g. time to work), schools for children, lifestyle preferences, and more. Outputs must be trustworthy (especially for big brands where bad hallucinations are unacceptable), and explainable — people need to know why a home was suggested.

Three pillars of data

  1. The seeker — Intent from structured integrations, unstructured descriptions, and behaviour (not everything is in neat fields).
  2. Place — Schools, shops, green space, transport, amenities — with care for how metrics are defined (e.g. density vs “quality” can encode bias).
  3. The business — Advertisers and commercial goals; the system must still allow controlled promotion without pretending everything is “pure” organic rank.

How to learn about the user

Long multi-step forms (high friction) still exist. Increasingly: light conversational steps (“two beds, garden, budget, commute, shops nearby…”), saves/likes, swipe UIs, and implicit signals — time on listing, which photos they linger on — to infer preferences.

Location and listings

Example: map radius around a home showing kindergartens — proximity and count matter for family buyers. Warning: naive use of correlated stats (e.g. crime vs density) can bake in bias; validate data and experiments.

Computer vision

Extract structured tags from photos (living room, open plan, “cozy” features, large windows) → scores; infer missing attributes (e.g. natural light) from what is visible.

Sponsorship vs relevance

Both can coexist: a recommendation engine with transparent business logic — hard boosts for paid placements plus feature-based matching on user needs. You control the rules.

End-to-end matching

Natural-language ask maps to your data model (beds, commute, style, schools, light, etc.). Combine user vector, property vector, neighbourhood signals → match score plus human-readable reasons (“great for family life” tied to earlier questions about schools).

What’s live in the market

NL search that surfaces inferred filters with yes/no confirmation. Some products deep-link into big consumer AIs — can bring traffic but may cede discovery. Fully conversational search. Hybrid products: structured questions + curated results, then a chat alongside results that refines based on likes/dislikes and follow-ups — lots of experimentation; winners not settled.

Q&A highlights

Is the strategic play one-off data ingestion or ongoing data / recommendations?

If you believe the game is the latter (continuous recommendation quality), partner with operators who own freshness and QA — many sources, cross-checks, regular validation. Geography keeps changing (new schools, new areas). Building that as a core competency is expensive; not every portal should try to own the whole pipeline.

Five years out — AI-native vs incumbents with brand + AI?

Hard to predict. Behaviour is shifting; neighbourhood-based recommendations are becoming more mainstream. The next edge may be deep, updating user profiles — learning continuously from behaviour, not a one-shot “here are suburbs with good schools.” Portals that truly understand the individual over time can build a moat (“they invested in learning me”).

How do you assess “good for schools” when someone searches?

Multiple factors: proximity, optionality (one good school vs several), admissions / oversubscription where data exists, and educational outcomes if available. Example: working with education data to model catchment-style attendance from pupil addresses for primary schools at scale; secondary datasets differ. Feeds can include admissions pressure and other criteria where you have them.
