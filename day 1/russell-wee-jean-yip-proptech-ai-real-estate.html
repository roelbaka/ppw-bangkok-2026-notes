Jean Yip Group — context

  • Family business (~1982 origin per session); major Singapore player; property development arm from ~2002.
  • 100+ projects across Singapore, Japan, Australia, UK, Malaysia (scale: large residential / mixed-use — e.g. ~20-storey towers, multi-country pipeline).
  • Operator + developer: Unlike many pure sellers, they often hold and operate (rental / long-term asset view) — not forced to sell every unit; can sell when it fits. Full value chain including sales via agents where needed.

What developers care about

“I’m not here to play with toys — I’m here to improve my P&L.” (Paraphrase.) Last “AI” use he mentions casually: Claude for a Thailand visa form — i.e. not living in demo-land.
  • First principles: Cost of construction, cost of land / discovery (acquisitions), cost of capital — not features on a slide.
  • Vendor gap: Too much pitch on technology, not enough on how it moves my P&L and my customer outcome. Less a “tech problem” than a translation / articulation problem — still sounds too “technological.”
  • AI stance: Open to AI-assisted tools (e.g. construction / settlement / asset management efficiency) — not AI-for-AI’s-sake. Must show concrete $ impact.
  • Anecdote: Pitch about differentiation / “better people” — pushback: “Show me one deal in this room.” Tie product to a specific business / P&L line, not abstract superiority.

Why big SaaS bets feel far away (Singapore lens)

  • Developer margins squeezed: e.g. from ~20% (2010s) toward ~10% now (order of magnitude per transcript).
  • Construction costs up materially (e.g. ~14% over five years cited).
  • Long cycles: land paid years before revenue; need strong margin on top of bid — so an 8–9 figure “experiment” on unproven software rarely feels urgent vs operational necessity.
  • Investor vs developer tension: Developers want to de-risk; investors want returns — adoption needs a very clear case unless you’re “playing with toys” that accidentally pay back.

Smart buildings / BMS / PM systems / yield & pricing tools — all fine if the path to yield ↑ or opex ↓ is modelled.

Portals & distribution (Singapore / Asia)

  • Model often consortium + contracted agents to sell stock — not “log into portal as primary channel” for every decision.
  • On acquisitions, relationship and network first; portals more “last resort” after network — strong product still often direct / relationship-led.
  • Implication for PropTech / portals: “How do you create value for me?” → must map to P&L and be modellable; great product alone isn’t enough if the buyer can’t see the line item.

Who decides? Relationships (Asia)

  • Often not the C-suite person in the room — chairman / family. ~70% of Singapore property orgs described as family-shaped; ~60% family-owned flavour in SE Asia (rough figures from talk).
  • Pitch may miss the real decider; need an internal champion with political capital.
  • Western vs Asian go-to-market: Don’t underestimate relationship and sincerity — first-gen leaders may not pitch-perfect English but read intent fast. Build for long trust, not only product deck.

Sales cycle: ambiguity + patience

  • Western enterprise software: often ~18 months to meaningful start.
  • Asia: May get no clear “no” for ~2 years — silence, endless “pilot,” “dinner next week,” string along — soft no vs direct rejection.
  • Even a yes can take 18+ months to real revenue.
  • Upside: Once in, sticky — “once you start you don’t really stop”; reinvestment and deep GP/LP-style relationships (example: large shared commitment / nine-figure scale in Japan with a partner — per transcript).

Closing — “The market still has a lot of potential”

At the end of the session he shifted tone: after the blunt P&L and Asia-GTM reality check, he stressed that this is not a reason to be pessimistic about PropTech or the region.

Upside for the room — The market has enormous potential: real estate and development in Asia / Southeast Asia remain huge, fragmented, and still under-digitised in many workflows (sales, ops, construction adjacency, asset management). For founders and platforms that translate value to the balance sheet and invest in relationships, the addressable opportunity is very large — the hard part is execution and patience, not lack of demand over time.
  • Framing: Tough buyer ≠ dead market; developers are selective, not closed.
  • Message to PropTech: The ceiling for better tools, data, and AI-assisted ops in property is high — if you match how the industry actually buys and decides.
