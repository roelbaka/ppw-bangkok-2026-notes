Talk summary (voice memo)

Retail media — what it is

  • Classic virtual staging is often a cost per image for the portal. PropTexx bridges staging + AI with retail brands embedded in those images — turning photos into a revenue line, not only an expense.
  • Analogy: evolution of display ads / CPM — but applied to property photos (high engagement inventory that wasn’t monetised this way before).
  • Retailers supply huge SKU catalogues (kitchens, furniture, appliances); PropTexx places real product visuals into virtually staged rooms. Portals = scale and attention; retailers pay for placement in context (“your kitchens in 100,000 kitchen photos in market X”).
  • Consumer journey: Original listing photo → AI restyle / themes (e.g. Mediterranean, alpine) → branded or retailer furniture → optional path to product selection / commerce. Formats: subtle branding, hotspots, still early; retailer interest is strong.

Portals & commercial models

  • Revenue share with portals — models vary. Some portals want full control (own media team, existing retailer relationships); others want PropTexx to run more of the stack.
  • Europe vs US-style commerce: Some retailers want click-out / add-to-basket; many European portals resist sending traffic off-site — keep users in the portal frame. Journey is phased: not extracting “maximum” retail revenue day one; over time some may allow click-outs.
  • Cost to portal: In high-demand markets, outputs can be subsidised or “free” to the portal, offset by retail spend. Rollout often starts with segmentation (e.g. ~30% of images), then expand retail insertion.
  • Portal upside: Longer time on site, engagement vs static galleries; staging shifts from cost to revenue; ties to Russell’s “P&L needle” (see Q&A).
  • Pricing narrative for retailers: Beyond dying CPM — CPMA (cost per minute of attention) and sponsor an image; retailers focus on unit economics.
  • Private sellers (Europe): Bundle with staging / image uplift; strong interest; large portals bundling packages, penetration building (e.g. mid-teens % first-year cited in discussion).

Product & UX

  • API-first: Images processed as listings are created; gallery often swipe before/after. When user enlarges a photo and gets an option to engage with AI output, engagement is high.
  • Scout24-style: Multiple themes, regenerated sets — presentation layer. Future: text prompts (“make it blue”, “this sofa”) → rotating ad / behaviour signals for retailers.
  • Widget: Embeddable experience (own page / script) for interaction without losing portal context where required.
  • Future / roadmap: User ingests own furniture (photo of existing sofa) to see it in the listing — “moving home” use case; plus retailer products in same scene.

Accuracy vs creativity (expanded)

  • Pipeline: prescreen / grade photo, room type, condition → generative step with structural integrity controls (no absurd hallucinations) → QC. Go-live failures (e.g. broken geometry) would trigger agent backlash.
  • Trade-off with customers: structural integrity vs creativity — often not both at once; as models improve, they may converge.
  • MLS US: Work on misrepresentation / rules; legal awareness varies by region. 3D / layout understanding to place furniture correctly; ~40 attributes per image cited for solid output — also becomes portal intelligence / data asset.

Ad load & trust

  • Partnership rules: start unobtrusive; avoid “advertising fest”; steady state before competing brands in one room, etc.

Q&A — questions & answers (app + session)

1. Vietnam / fake listings & sustainable retail media

Q: Emerging markets, fake listings vs Australia — advice for retail media that maintains user trust?

A: Two layers: (1) Listing quality / trust — watermark detection, duplicates, policy tooling; country-specific legal levers; identify fakes and action plans to reduce volume. (2) Retail layer can be hybrid: even if some listings are weak, the interactive image experience can still add value for the portal (time on site, engagement) — retail isn’t only “buy furniture for this exact home.” Responsibility for listing integrity remains with the portal.

2. Russell’s P&L “needle” — has PropTexx moved it?

Q: Extent of client impact?

A: AI can move top line (e.g. search) — does it drive leads/revenue? Staging used to be pure cost; retail model turns it into revenue. On portals with hundreds of millions in revenue, share is still to be determined — but clear positives on smaller examples (e.g. modest CRA uplift → meaningful extra monthly revenue cited). Large European focus: private-seller bundles, staging penetration growing. For retailers, it’s a new channel — product in situ vs only store or roadside — from IKEA-scale to local retailers; heavy SKU onboarding complexity.

3. Image AI — accuracy or creativity?

A: See pipeline above: structural integrity first for production use; creativity pushes against that — customer choice on the dial. MLS / misrepresentation sensitivity in US; regional variation.

4. Lagged journey — sofa after you buy the house — track conversion?

A: Trackable links / affiliate-style click-through to retailer sites for shoppable placements — measurable to a point. Full end-to-end furniture purchase through the portal is not fully built; discussions ongoing. Tension: retailers want more traffic / multiple on conversion; portals reluctant to send users away — tradeoff.
